Sound

Chapter 11 | Physics

Summary

This chapter discusses the production, propagation, and characteristics of sound waves, including reflection, echo, and the human hearing range.

‚Ä¢ Production and Propagation of Sound

Sound is produced by vibrations. It travels as a wave (longitudinal in air) and requires a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to propagate.

[Image illustrating longitudinal sound wave]

‚Ä¢ Characteristics of Sound Waves

  • Wavelength ($\lambda$): Distance between two consecutive compressions or rarefactions.
  • Frequency ($\nu$): Number of oscillations per unit time (Unit: Hertz, Hz).
  • Time Period ($T$): Time taken for one complete oscillation. ($T = 1/\nu$)
  • Speed ($v$): $v = \nu \times \lambda$
[Image of a labeled sound wave (compression, rarefaction, wavelength)]

‚Ä¢ Reflection of Sound and Echoes

Sound bounces off a surface, giving rise to echoes.

  • Echo ‚Äì reflected sound heard after the original sound
  • For distinct echo: distance between source and reflector $\geq 17.2 \text{ m}$ (for air, speed $= 344 \text{ m/s}$)

‚Ä¢ Applications of Reflection of Sound

  • Echo sounding (SONAR) ‚Äì used to detect underwater objects, measure depth
  • Megaphones and auditoriums use sound reflection to amplify sound
[Image of SONAR application]

‚Ä¢ Range of Hearing

Humans can hear frequencies from $20 \text{ Hz}$ to $20,000 \text{ Hz}$. Sounds above $20 \text{ kHz}$ are ultrasonic; below $20 \text{ Hz}$ are infrasonic.

‚Ä¢ Musical Instruments

Produce sound of definite pitch through vibrating strings, air columns, or membranes.

‚Ä¢ Speed of Sound in Different Mediums

Sound travels fastest in solids, then liquids, and slowest in gases.

‚Ä¢ Fun Facts

  • Bats use ultrasonic sound waves to navigate in dark.
  • Dolphins communicate using sound underwater.
  • Thunder is the sound produced due to rapid expansion of air during lightning.
